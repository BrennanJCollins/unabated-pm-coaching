## How This File Works

Each entry has a consistent structure:
- **Term**: The canonical name
- **Definition**: Brennan's definition in his voice
- **Parent**: If this concept lives under a larger framework
- **Context**: When/where a PM encounters this
- **Related skills**: Which SKILL.md files reference it
- **Good/Better/Best**: Progression examples where applicable (not a standalone concept; it's the evaluation method used across all skills)

Skills should reference terms by their exact canonical name. If a skill uses an alternate name, add it as an alias.

## Core Frameworks

### Four Pillars of Product Management
**Definition:** The four areas every PM operates across. Strategy (narrow market chaos to a focused bet), Delivery (expand that bet into a validated solution), Customer Experience (make it effortless to adopt and use), Growth (drive business impact and scale). They look linear but you work across all four depending on product maturity.
**Context:** The diagnostic lens for everything. Every skill, every audit score, every coaching conversation maps back to these four.
**Related skills:** four-pillars-check, career-transition-navigator, and referenced across all 27 skills
**Aliases:** Four Pillars, The Pillars

### Bridge Framework
**Definition:** Connect discovered user needs to buyer and company metrics that matter. Show the relationship between feature decisions, areas of impact, and the research data supporting the decision. Four steps: (1) user and buyer interviews, (2) customer journey mapping, (3) product metrics ladder from user needs to buyer metrics, (4) hypotheses to affect user needs that lead to buyer impact, and user story mapping to visualize.
**Context:** When you need to build the full case from "users told us X" to "here's why the business should invest." The Bridge is the complete workflow. The Product Metrics Ladder is one tool inside it (step 3).
**Related skills:** bridge-builder, user-buyer-bridge
**Aliases:** The Bridge, Bridge Builder

### Product Metrics Ladder
**Definition:** Answering "why are we doing what we're doing?" by asking questions like "what is this feature intended to change for users?", "if users can now do that better, what are they able to achieve?", "why does that matter to their life or company?", and "if we are able to help them achieve that outcome, what will that do for our business?" The structure: Feature → user task or behavior change → user value → buyer outcome → business outcome. PMs can create as many layers as necessary to break the goal or problem down into something small enough to directly affect.
**Context:** Step 3 of the Bridge Framework. Also used standalone when a PM needs to justify a feature or connect a metric to a business outcome. Works bidirectionally: top-down (what outcome do we need?) and bottom-up (what does this feature actually change?).
**Related skills:** product-metrics-ladder, bridge-builder, product-objective-definition
**Aliases:** Metrics Ladder, The Ladder

### Hypothesis Framework
**Definition:** Framing product work as testable bets instead of requirements. The full template: "We believe that if we helped [user persona] do [user task] better, then that would help them achieve [user goal] and hit [user success metric]. By improving [user success metric], their company would improve [department KPI], which would lead to achieving [Company Objective]."
**Context:** Replaces traditional user stories and requirements documents. Forces the PM to connect the user task all the way up to the company objective before writing a single line of spec. If you can't fill in every bracket, you don't understand the problem well enough yet.
**Related skills:** product-objective-definition, strategic-storytelling-pitch, strategy-articulation, bridge-builder, stakeholder-request-reframe
**Aliases:** Bridge Madlib, Hypothesis User Story

### Strategy Assessment Criteria
**Definition:** Six criteria for evaluating whether a product strategy is sound:
(1) **Pervasive Problem** - Evidence of similar need across many customers
(2) **Urgent Problem** - Demonstrated willingness to pay to solve today
(3) **Differentiated** - A solution that's 10x better than existing alternatives that we are well-suited to create
(4) **Unique Advantage** - Proven capabilities and approaches already within the organization to create a long-term moat
(5) **Company Priority** - Focus areas for alignment and growth across the organization, rolls up to business strategy
(6) **Future Potential** - Investment in innovation for emerging markets with huge growth potential, compounding results over time
**Context:** Your strategy should be able to answer these questions: Which customers first? Which problem first? Which customer capabilities first? What do we say "not yet" to? (The vision says "no.") WHY?!
**Related skills:** strategy-articulation, vision-synthesis
**Aliases:** Strategy Pressure Test

### Order Taker to Outcome Owner
**Definition:** The progression from executing what others decide to owning business outcomes. An order taker waits to be told what to build. An outcome owner investigates problems, sets direction, and takes responsibility for results. The shift requires an ownership mindset: thinking "I own this business and its outcomes" instead of "I manage my feature."
**Context:** The foundational mindset shift the entire course is built on. If a PM is still operating as an order taker, no framework or technique will matter until this changes. The ownership mindset is the tactic that unlocks the shift.
**Related skills:** order-taker-to-outcome-owner, four-pillars-check
**Aliases:** Ownership Mindset, Employee vs. Owner Mindset

### Vision Tests
**Definition:** Four tests to evaluate whether a product vision is actually useful:
(1) **Boring Test** - Is it compelling enough to inspire action, or could any company say it?
(2) **Clarity Test** - Can your team explain it without reading a slide?
(3) **Belief Test** - Would your team bet their careers on it being achievable?
(4) **Decision Test** - Does it help you say no to things that don't fit?
**Context:** Applied after drafting a vision statement. Most visions fail the Boring Test (too generic) or the Decision Test (too vague to guide tradeoffs).
**Related skills:** vision-synthesis, strategy-articulation

## Stakeholder & Influence Concepts

### Stakeholder Judo
**Definition:** Take the energy stakeholders bring and redirect it instead of fighting it. When someone demands a feature, don't say yes (lose focus) or no (create conflict). Instead, use sincere curiosity to understand what they actually need, then reframe their request as supporting your strategy. Reposition, rephrase, reframe.
**Context:** Used when a stakeholder shows up with a feature request, an executive has a "great idea," or sales is pushing for a one-off. Requires having your Metrics Ladder built before the conversation starts. You can't do this in the moment without preparation.
**Related skills:** stakeholder-request-reframe, strategic-storytelling-pitch, cafe-update
**Aliases:** Reposition/Rephrase/Reframe

### Hidden Metrics
**Definition:** The unspoken metrics each stakeholder actually cares about but won't put on a slide. A CEO worries about competitive position. A CFO worries about margin erosion. A VP of Sales worries about quota. An engineering director worries about technical debt slowing the team. Influential PMs identify these before pitching, not during.
**Context:** Used in pitching, stakeholder management, and organizational navigation. Combined with the "What Would Get Them Fired?" test to map the real power structure beyond the org chart.
**Related skills:** strategic-storytelling-pitch, cafe-update, first-90-days-playbook
**Aliases:** Hidden Incentives, Hidden Incentives Map

### "What Would Get Them Fired?" Test
**Definition:** The fastest way to understand what a stakeholder really cares about. Ask yourself: what outcome would put their job at risk? Tech leaders fear performance disasters. Revenue leaders fear missing quota. Finance fears budget overruns. Executives fear losing market position. The answer tells you which metrics to connect your pitch to.
**Context:** Used before any pitch, presentation, or stakeholder conversation. Part of the preparation that makes Stakeholder Judo and Strategic Storytelling work.
**Related skills:** strategic-storytelling-pitch, cafe-update, first-90-days-playbook, confidence-scenario-simulator

### Sincere Curiosity
**Definition:** Six diagnostic questions for responding to stakeholder feature requests: (1) What problem are you trying to solve? (2) For whom? (3) What have you tried? (4) What doesn't work? (5) What would success look like? (6) Why is this important right now?
**Context:** The first move in Stakeholder Judo. Before reframing anything, genuinely understand what the stakeholder needs. If you skip this step, your reframe will feel dismissive instead of strategic.
**Related skills:** stakeholder-request-reframe
**Aliases:** Six Diagnostic Questions

## Storytelling & Pitching

### Strategic Storytelling
**Definition:** A three-act structure for pitches that gets to "yes" by leading with emotion, not logic. Act 1: Human Stakes (make them feel the problem with a specific person, place, and cost). Act 2: Scale (bridge from one story to systemic pattern with data). Act 3: Solution (now obvious given the story). Built on the emotional + logical formula: we make decisions emotionally, then use numbers to rationalize them. If you lead with logic, your solution is knocking on a locked door.
**Context:** Used for internal pitches, strategy presentations, budget requests, and roadmap defenses. Different from demos (Five-Act) and hallway conversations (Cafe Update).
**Related skills:** strategic-storytelling-pitch
**Aliases:** Three-Act Structure, Three-Act Pitch, Emotional + Logical Formula

### Five-Act Demo Structure
**Definition:** Brennan's framework for product demos that close deals instead of touring features. Act 1 (Context: understand their world before showing yours). Act 2 (Proof: establish credibility with a relevant case). Act 3 (Journey: show a workflow solving their specific pain). Act 4 (Contrast: let THEM articulate the value, not you). Act 5 (Next Step: BAMFAM).
**Context:** Used for customer-facing product demos. The critical difference from a feature tour: in Act 4, the prospect says why this matters to them. If you're doing the talking in Act 4, you're doing it wrong.
**Related skills:** demo-script-reviewer
**Aliases:** Demo Structure

### Cafe Update
**Definition:** A 60-second structure for when a senior leader asks "how's it going?" in the hallway, elevator, or coffee line. Five parts: (1) Context (business problem they care about), (2) Win (specific customer proof point), (3) Insight (systemic pattern from that one win), (4) Ask (specific meeting request), (5) Close (graceful exit). If you don't have the ask ready, you wasted the moment.
**Context:** Spontaneous conversations with executives. You never know when you'll get 60 seconds with someone who can greenlight your initiative. Have this ready at all times.
**Related skills:** cafe-update

### BAMFAM
**Definition:** Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Never end a demo, pitch, or conversation without scheduling the next step while you're still in the room. The moment someone walks away, inertia wins.
**Context:** Act 5 of the Five-Act Demo Structure. Also applies to Cafe Updates (Part 4: the Ask).
**Related skills:** demo-script-reviewer, cafe-update

## Confidence & Influence

### Earned Confidence
**Parent concept for:** Competence → Credibility → Influence, Warmth-Power Balance, Imposter's Paradox, Quiet Expert's Trap
**Definition:** Competence + Confidence = Influence. You need both. Substance without delivery means nobody hears your ideas. Delivery without substance means people stop trusting you. The goal is to build real competence first, then develop the confidence to communicate it effectively.
**Context:** The framework for understanding why smart PMs get overlooked and why charismatic PMs eventually get found out.
**Related skills:** confidence-scenario-simulator, impostor-to-strategist

### Competence → Credibility → Influence
**Parent:** Earned Confidence
**Definition:** The time-ordered path to real influence, especially in a new role. First demonstrate competence (small visible wins). Then earn credibility (a pattern of being right). Then lead (you've earned the right to direct). You can't skip steps. Trying to influence before establishing credibility creates resistance.
**Context:** Used in first 90 days of a new PM role, after a promotion, or when joining a new team.
**Related skills:** first-90-days-playbook, confidence-scenario-simulator

### Warmth-Power Balance
**Parent:** Earned Confidence
**Definition:** Too much power without warmth is intimidating. Too much warmth without power is ineffectual. Influential PMs calibrate both: speak with authority (slower pace, strategic pauses, statements not questions) while maintaining genuine warmth (open posture, active listening, real interest in others).
**Context:** Applied in high-stakes conversations, presentations, and stakeholder management.
**Related skills:** confidence-scenario-simulator

### Imposter's Paradox
**Parent:** Earned Confidence
**Definition:** Confidence without competence. When someone presents well but lacks substance. Eventually gets exposed.
**Context:** One of the two failure modes of Earned Confidence. Less common in PMs who seek coaching (they usually have the opposite problem).
**Related skills:** confidence-scenario-simulator, impostor-to-strategist

### Quiet Expert's Trap
**Parent:** Earned Confidence
**Definition:** Competence without confidence. Having the right answers but staying invisible because you can't communicate them effectively. The more common failure mode for experienced PMs.
**Context:** If you're the PM who always knows the answer but someone else gets credit for saying it louder, you're in this trap.
**Related skills:** confidence-scenario-simulator, impostor-to-strategist

## Interview & Research Concepts

### Nine Principles for Interview Questions
**Definition:** Brennan's framework for designing customer interview questions that uncover real problems instead of validating product ideas: (1) Ask about their lives, not your product. (2) Focus on Before/During/After. (3) Behavioral, not hypothetical. (4) Quantify pain. (5) Connect to business drivers. (6) Willingness-to-pay proxy. (7) Distinguish buyer from user. (8) Drill-down methodology. (9) Skin in the game validation.
**Context:** Used before any customer interview. If your questions include "would you use X?" or "what do you think of our approach?", you're validating your product, not discovering their problem.
**Related skills:** interview-question-design

### Skin in the Game Validation
**Definition:** End customer interviews with a test of real interest, not hypothetical enthusiasm. "Would you be willing to be contacted when we have something to show?" "Can you refer others with this problem?" "Would you consider a pilot?" More credible than "would you use this?"
**Context:** Principle 9 of Interview Question Design. The difference between someone being polite and someone being a potential customer.
**Related skills:** interview-question-design

### N of 1 Thinking
**Definition:** One person with acute pain who would pay to solve it today is worth more than a hundred people with mild interest. Focus on finding real buyers, not validating product ideas at scale.
**Context:** Guides early customer discovery. Stop surveying 500 people. Find one who would write a check.
**Related skills:** interview-question-design

## Business & Economics Concepts

### Standalone Company Test
**Definition:** Could this product sustain a standalone business, or does it only work as a subsidized feature of a larger company? If the answer is no, the business model isn't viable.
**Context:** Applied when evaluating Business Model Canvas. Exposes products that look profitable but only because the parent company absorbs their costs.
**Related skills:** business-model-canvas

### Starving Crowd Test
**Definition:** Is there a crowd of people actively trying to solve this problem right now? If they're not hungry for a solution, there's no market. The best products solve problems people are already spending money and time trying to fix.
**Context:** Customer segment validation in Business Model Canvas. If you have to convince people they have a problem, you're in trouble.
**Related skills:** business-model-canvas

### 10X Rule
**Definition:** The value your product creates for the customer should be at least 10X the price. If the ROI only shows 2X, you're either missing value drivers or the price is too high. A 10X case sells itself. A 2X case starts a negotiation.
**Context:** Used in ROI model building and pricing validation. If you can't show 10X, go find the missing value drivers before the pitch.
**Related skills:** roi-calculator-builder, bridge-builder

### Right-Side-First Rule
**Definition:** When building a Business Model Canvas, start with Revenue Streams and work backward. Most PMs start with Cost Structure or Value Proposition. Starting with revenue forces you to think like a business owner: who pays, how much, and why?
**Context:** A specific tactical instruction for Business Model Canvas creation. Reflects the ownership mindset shift.
**Related skills:** business-model-canvas

## Career & Resume Concepts

### Biography Trap
**Definition:** A resume anti-pattern where bullets read like a biography of tasks ("managed," "coordinated," "led") instead of telling strategic stories about how you think. The hiring manager learns what you did but not how you decided or why it mattered.
**Context:** The most common resume failure for experienced PMs. They have great experience but present it as a task list instead of a strategy portfolio.
**Related skills:** resume-as-product-coach

### Language Reset
**Definition:** Replace "requirements" with "hypotheses." Replace "the business wants" with "we've observed." Replace "we need to ship" with "we need to validate." The words you use signal whether you think like an order taker or an outcome owner.
**Context:** Used in first 90 days of a new role and throughout career development. Small vocabulary changes signal big mindset shifts.
**Related skills:** first-90-days-playbook, product-objective-definition

### Feature Parity Convergence
**Definition:** AI-generated PM outputs (PRDs, competitive analyses, user research summaries) are becoming commoditized. Every PM using the same LLMs produces similar output. AI adoption is table stakes, not a differentiator. What differentiates is human ingenuity: asking questions nobody else is asking, spotting patterns across domains, making judgment calls with incomplete data.
**Context:** Guides how PMs should think about AI in their careers. Stop trying to be faster with AI. Start being more insightful with it.
**Related skills:** ai-career-impact-advisor

### Diamond Moment
**Definition:** A PM's highest-leverage insight: the moment when cross-domain pattern recognition produces something no one else in the room could see. This is what AI can't replace. It comes from the intersection of customer empathy, technical understanding, business context, and market awareness.
**Context:** The aspirational target for PM career development. Build toward having more of these moments.
**Related skills:** ai-career-impact-advisor

## Situational Tools

### Six Stakeholder Archetypes
**Definition:** Six challenging personas used in high-stakes conversation practice: The Skeptic (questions everything), The Interrupter (won't let you finish), The Tangent Puller (derails to side topics), The Number Cruncher (demands data for every claim), The Friendly Doubter (agrees but undermines), The Silent Observer (says nothing, just watches).
**Context:** Used in confidence scenario simulation. Each archetype tests a different skill. The Silent Observer is often the hardest because there's no feedback to read.
**Related skills:** confidence-scenario-simulator, unabatedpm-interview-coach

### Three-Quarter Rule
**Definition:** Most career transitioners already have 3 out of 4 pillars covered from their previous career. Engineers have Delivery. Designers have Customer Experience. Consultants have Strategy. The job isn't starting from scratch. It's identifying the one gap and closing it.
**Context:** Used in career transition coaching. Reduces the feeling of "I don't know anything about PM" to "I need to build one skill area."
**Related skills:** career-transition-navigator
**Aliases:** 3/4 Rule

## Notes for Skill Authors

When referencing glossary terms in SKILL.md files or audit reports:
1. Use the exact canonical name (e.g., "Product Metrics Ladder" not "metrics ladder" or "the ladder")
2. If a term has a parent, reference the parent on first use (e.g., "Quiet Expert's Trap, a failure mode of Earned Confidence")
3. Good/Better/Best is NOT a standalone glossary term. It's the evaluation method. Use it as a scoring pattern, not as a concept to define.
4. Industry-standard frameworks (JTBD, Kano, BMC, Mom Test) don't need glossary definitions. PMs should know these or can look them up.
